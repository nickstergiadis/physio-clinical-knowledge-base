## 1. Clinical summary
Common presentation in primary care. Most episodes improve, but recurrence is common. Active management and graded activity are central.

## 2. Key presentation patterns
- Local lumbar pain with or without referred leg pain.
- Radicular pattern may include dermatomal pain, paraesthesia, and neural mechanosensitivity.

## 3. Differential diagnosis
- Hip pathology, SIJ-related pain, peripheral neuropathy, spinal stenosis, serious spinal pathology.

## 4. Red flags / referral triggers
- Progressive neurologic deficit, saddle anesthesia, bowel/bladder dysfunction, systemic illness, major trauma.

## 5. Subjective exam priorities
Irritability, directional preference, neurologic symptoms, functional limits, psychosocial barriers.

## 6. Objective exam priorities
Lumbar AROM, repeated movement response, neuro screen (myotomes/dermatomes/reflexes), SLR/slump where indicated.

## 7. Useful special tests and their limits
SLR and slump help with neural mechanosensitivity but are not standalone diagnostic tests for root compression.

## 8. Common impairments / comparable signs
Movement fear, reduced trunk/hip endurance, neural sensitivity, deconditioning.

## 9. Management principles
- Exercise and activity continuation are first-line.
- Manual therapy can be adjunctive for short-term symptom modulation.
- Address sleep, fear, and pacing.

## 10. Exercise progression logic
Start with tolerated movement exposure, then trunk/hip capacity, then task-specific loading and conditioning.

## 11. Patient education priorities
Natural history, flare-up planning, stay active, symptom-guided loading, when to seek urgent care.

## 12. Outcome measures
ODI, PSFS, NPRS.

## 13. Escalation / imaging / injection / surgical considerations
Imaging is not routine early unless red flags or severe progressive deficits. Escalate if persistent disabling radiculopathy or neurologic decline.

## 14. Evidence update notes
Evidence update needed: align with latest radicular pain guideline updates and stratified care models.

## 15. Practical clinic pearls
Centralization/peripheralization response can guide loading direction but should be integrated with full clinical reasoning.
