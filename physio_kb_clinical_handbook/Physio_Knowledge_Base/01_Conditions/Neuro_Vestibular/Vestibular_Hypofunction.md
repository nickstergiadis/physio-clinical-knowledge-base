## 1. Clinical summary
Peripheral vestibular hypofunction presents with dizziness, oscillopsia, and balance deficits. Targeted vestibular rehab is first-line.

## 2. Key presentation patterns
Worse with head movement, visual motion sensitivity, gait instability, blurred vision during walking/head turns.

## 3. Differential diagnosis
BPPV, central vestibular disorders, vestibular migraine, orthostatic causes, medication effects.

## 4. Red flags / referral triggers
Acute focal neurologic signs, severe headache, dysarthria, ataxia out of proportion, new hearing loss with neuro signs.

## 5. Subjective exam priorities
Onset pattern, triggers, fall history, visual dependence, migraine/cardiovascular history.

## 6. Objective exam priorities
VOR testing, dynamic visual acuity, gait/balance under sensory challenge, positional testing as indicated.

## 7. Useful special tests and their limits
Head impulse supports peripheral loss but examiner skill affects sensitivity.

## 8. Common impairments / comparable signs
Gaze stability deficit, postural instability, reduced confidence/mobility.

## 9. Management principles
Gaze stabilization, habituation, balance retraining, progressive exposure to movement and environments.

## 10. Exercise progression logic
Short frequent VOR doses -> complex head/body tasks -> dual-task/community mobility.

## 11. Patient education priorities
Temporary symptom provocation during exercises is expected; underdosing slows adaptation.

## 12. Outcome measures
DHI, ABC scale, TUG, gait speed.

## 13. Escalation / imaging / injection / surgical considerations
Urgent referral for central signs or unexplained acute neurologic progression.
