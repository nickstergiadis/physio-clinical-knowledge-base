# Hip Osteoarthritis (Hip OA)

## Clinical summary
Hip OA is commonly diagnosed clinically in adults with activity-related hip pain and short-duration morning stiffness. Imaging is not routinely required when the presentation is typical.

## Typical presentation
- Groin, anterior thigh, buttock, or lateral hip pain
- Stiffness, especially after inactivity
- Walking, stairs, footwear, transfers, and prolonged standing/walking become limited
- Reduced ROM, often internal rotation first
- Symptoms may fluctuate with load and flare periods

## Subjective findings to prioritize
- Activity-related pain pattern
- Morning stiffness duration
- Walking and ADL limitations
- Response to previous exercise or activity modification
- Irritability and flare behaviour
- Weight, comorbidity, and analgesic context
- Differential screen for lumbar referral, fracture, inflammatory disease, malignancy, AVN

## Objective findings
- Gait
- Hip ROM, especially IR/flexion/extension
- Strength and functional task tolerance
- Sit-to-stand, squat, step tasks, single-leg tolerance as appropriate
- Compare symptom irritability vs deconditioning burden
- Identify whether major limitation is pain, stiffness, weakness, load intolerance, or fear

## First-line management
- Information and support
- Therapeutic exercise tailored to needs
- Encourage consistent long-term adherence
- Weight management support where relevant
- Manual therapy only as an adjunct to exercise, not a stand-alone strategy
- Simple mobility and strength work linked to function:
  - walking tolerance
  - sit-to-stand
  - stairs
  - footwear / floor transfers
  - balance and gait confidence where relevant

## Practical exercise priorities
- maintain or improve ROM within tolerance
- hip/trunk strengthening
- aerobic capacity
- gait and transfer practice
- flare-aware dose adjustment rather than stop/start inactivity cycles

## Treatments not to overuse
- imaging for routine diagnosis/follow-up when the picture is typical
- passive care without exercise
- electrotherapy modalities with poor evidence
- long-term reliance on medication without active management

## When to refer
Consider referral for joint replacement discussion when:
- pain, stiffness, or functional loss is substantially impacting quality of life
- non-surgical care has been ineffective or unsuitable
- diagnosis is uncertain
- deformity or rapid deterioration is progressing
