# Femoroacetabular Impingement Syndrome (FAIS)

## Clinical summary
FAIS is a motion-related hip disorder characterized by hip pain, clinical findings, and imaging features that reflect cam, pincer, or mixed morphology. Not everyone with morphology is symptomatic.

## Typical presentation
- Groin/anterior hip pain
- Pain with deep flexion, squatting, sitting, pivoting, cutting, twisting
- Reduced hip flexion and/or internal rotation
- May report sharp catching or pinching, but mechanical symptoms are not mandatory
- Often active or athletic, but not exclusively

## Subjective findings to prioritize
- Pain location: groin, anterior hip, C-sign
- Irritability with sitting, squatting, stairs, deep flexion
- Training load / sport demands
- Aggravating positions: flexion-adduction-internal rotation patterns
- Loss of gym or sport tolerance
- Response to prior rehab and whether it was targeted
- Mechanical symptoms, night pain, instability, non-MSK features

## Objective findings
- Hip ROM, especially flexion and internal rotation
- Provocation testing in impingement positions
- Strength: abductors, extensors, external rotators, trunk
- Single-leg control, squat mechanics, step tasks
- Lumbar, pelvic, adductor, and abdominal differential screen
- Consider generalized hypermobility where relevant

## Differential diagnosis
- Hip OA
- labral/chondral pathology
- iliopsoas-related pain
- adductor-related groin pain
- athletic pubalgia / abdominal wall pain
- referred lumbar pain
- stress injury / fracture
- inflammatory or non-MSK pathology

## First-line conservative management
- Education on the condition and symptom modulation
- Activity modification to reduce repeated impingement provocation without unnecessary deconditioning
- Progressive strengthening of:
  - hip abductors
  - extensors
  - rotators
  - trunk / lumbopelvic control
- Movement retraining:
  - squat pattern
  - landing/cutting strategy
  - single-leg control
- ROM work only within tolerance; avoid aggressive impingement stretching in irritable patients
- Consider manual therapy as an adjunct, not a stand-alone plan

## Practical exercise priorities
### Early / irritable
- symptom calming
- modify sitting depth and squat depth
- isometric or low-irritability strengthening
- trunk and pelvic control drills

### Mid stage
- progress resistance
- restore functional single-leg control
- improve hip/trunk capacity
- graded return to gym and field/court tasks

### Late stage
- plyometric and change-of-direction progressions as indicated
- sport-specific exposure
- repeated-load tolerance
- return-to-performance criteria

## What tends to help most
- specific strengthening
- education
- movement control work
- load management

## What to avoid over-relying on
- passive treatment alone
- repeated painful end-range impingement stretching
- overmedicalizing asymptomatic morphology
- imaging without clinical context

## When to refer
Consider medical / surgical opinion when:
- diagnosis remains uncertain
- symptoms remain meaningfully limiting after an adequate rehab trial
- patient fails to improve after a well-dosed, targeted program
- substantial mechanical symptoms persist
- goals require return to high-demand sport and function remains capped despite good rehab adherence
