# Greater Trochanteric Pain Syndrome (GTPS) / Gluteal Tendinopathy

## Clinical summary
GTPS is a lateral hip pain syndrome commonly involving gluteal tendon pathology with or without associated bursal irritation. The old “trochanteric bursitis” label is often too narrow.

## Typical presentation
- Lateral hip pain over the greater trochanter
- Pain lying on the affected side
- Pain with walking, hills, stairs, prolonged standing, single-leg stance
- Pain with crossing legs or hanging into hip adduction
- May radiate down the lateral thigh but usually not below the knee in a clear radicular pattern

## Subjective findings to prioritize
- Side-lying and sleep tolerance
- Single-leg loading tolerance
- Stairs, hills, prolonged walking/standing
- Sitting posture and crossed-leg habits
- Training load changes
- Lumbar symptoms and overlap
- Tendon tear suspicion: major weakness, marked gait deviation, abrupt change after overload/trauma

## Objective findings
- Palpation over the greater trochanter / gluteal tendon insertion region
- Resisted hip abduction and other lateral hip load tests
- Single-leg stance tolerance / symptom provocation
- Trendelenburg pattern or frontal-plane control deficits
- Hip abductor strength and pain response
- ROM screen with attention to adduction/compression sensitivity
- Lumbar screen where indicated

## Differential diagnosis
- referred lumbar pain
- hip OA
- FAIS
- gluteal tendon tear
- external snapping hip
- proximal hamstring or deep gluteal pain
- less commonly: stress fracture, inflammatory or non-MSK causes

## Conservative management
- Education first:
  - explain tendon-related loading
  - reduce compression-dominant positions
  - modify side-lying, standing posture, crossed legs, and cumulative load
- Progressive abductor loading:
  - start with tolerable isometrics or low-load work if irritable
  - progress to isotonic strengthening
  - later progress to heavier and functional single-leg loading
- Build frontal-plane pelvic control and trunk contribution
- Dose walking, stairs, hills, and sport gradually
- Use manual therapy only as an adjunct if it improves tolerance or movement confidence

## Compression-management points
Commonly useful advice:
- avoid hanging on one hip
- avoid prolonged hip adduction postures
- modify side sleeping with pillow support
- avoid aggressive adduction stretching in insertional tendon presentations

## Injection considerations
- Corticosteroid injection may help short term in selected patients
- It does not address the load-capacity problem
- Repeated injection should not replace rehabilitation
- Orthobiologic evidence remains mixed and context-dependent

## When to refer
Refer or seek further opinion if:
- major abductor weakness suggests tendon tear
- the patient cannot abduct effectively against gravity
- gait deviation is marked
- pain is non-mechanical or escalating unexpectedly
- there is failure to progress after appropriate load-based rehabilitation
- imaging is needed because tear severity or alternate pathology may change management
