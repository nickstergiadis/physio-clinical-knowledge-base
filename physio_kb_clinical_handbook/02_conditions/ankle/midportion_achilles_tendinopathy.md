# Midportion Achilles Tendinopathy

## Clinical summary
Midportion Achilles tendinopathy is a load-related tendon pain condition, usually presenting with localized pain 2–6 cm proximal to the tendon insertion. Current guidance supports progressive mechanical loading as the mainstay of treatment, with activity modification rather than complete rest.

## Typical presentation
- localized midportion Achilles pain, usually 2–6 cm proximal to insertion
- pain with running, hopping, skipping, calf raises, hills, or push-off tasks
- morning pain/stiffness may be present
- focal tenderness and possible tendon thickening
- onset may be gradual or follow abrupt load increase

## Key reasoning points
- separate midportion from insertional Achilles pain
- imaging changes can support context but do not define symptom severity on their own
- tendon loading tolerance and calf capacity are usually more clinically useful than imaging findings alone

## Subjective findings to prioritize
- exact pain location
- onset and training-load history
- current running / jumping / hill / gym exposure
- morning stiffness behavior
- response to tendon loading and to rest
- prior injection, imaging, orthoses, heel lifts, or shockwave history
- concern about rupture or “wearing out”

## Objective findings
- focal midportion tenderness
- calf raise pain and endurance
- hop or spring tolerance if appropriate
- plantarflexor strength / endurance
- ankle dorsiflexion and subtalar mobility where relevant
- palpation and functional loading response rather than over-reliance on imaging

## First-line conservative management
- progressive tendon-loading exercise
- continue meaningful activity within pain tolerance rather than full rest
- modify volume / intensity / hill / plyometric exposure strategically
- rebuild calf endurance and whole-limb capacity
- address relevant mobility or kinetic-chain deficits only if they meaningfully help function

## Exercise priorities
### Early phase
- pain-guided calf loading
- activity modification rather than shutdown
- establish a tolerable loading baseline
- isometric work may help some patients symptomatically, but should not replace progression

### Progressive phase
- heavy slow resistance or eccentric/concentric loading progression
- calf endurance work
- walking / running exposure planning
- progressive plyometric preparation where relevant

### Late phase
- return to faster elastic loading
- running, hills, jumping, sprinting, or sport-specific progression
- recurrence-prevention load planning

## Adjuncts / cautions
- stretching may help if dorsiflexion limitation is relevant
- manual therapy is adjunctive, not primary treatment
- complete rest is generally not indicated in nonacute presentations
- heel lifts / orthoses have mixed or insufficient evidence and should not replace loading rehab
- night splints are not recommended for routine use

## Referral / escalation
Consider referral when:
- rupture or partial tear is suspected
- diagnosis is uncertain
- symptoms fail to improve after a solid loading-based trial
- insertional pathology / plantaris / systemic inflammatory concern is stronger than a typical midportion picture

## Patient education points
- Tendons usually respond best to graded loading, not total rest.
- Pain during rehab must be interpreted over time, not only moment to moment.
- Recovery often takes time and load planning matters.
- Imaging abnormalities do not automatically mean severe damage.
