# Chronic Ankle Instability

## Clinical summary
Chronic ankle instability (CAI) describes ongoing symptoms after ankle sprain that can include recurrent sprains, repeated “giving way,” reduced confidence, and persistent balance / movement-coordination deficits. Conservative care remains first-line, with strong emphasis on sensorimotor rehabilitation, progressive strength work, and return-to-sport exposure.

## Typical presentation
- previous ankle sprain history
- repeated “giving way” or recurrent sprains
- reduced confidence on uneven ground, cutting, landing, or sport
- persistent stiffness, weakness, or reduced dorsiflexion
- balance deficits or poor single-leg control

## Key reasoning points
- CAI is not explained by laxity alone.
- Persistent symptoms often reflect sensorimotor and functional deficits.
- The clinical task is to decide whether the main problem is instability, pain, stiffness, strength deficit, confidence, or a combination.

## Subjective findings to prioritize
- number and timing of prior sprains
- true recurrent sprain vs instability sensation only
- uneven-ground and sport limitations
- brace / tape use
- footwear and activity context
- fear, avoidance, and confidence
- prior rehab completion or lack thereof

## Objective findings
- dynamic balance testing
- dorsiflexion mobility
- calf raise capacity
- peroneal / whole-limb strength
- hop / landing / deceleration tolerance when appropriate
- movement-quality deficits during single-leg tasks
- screening for alternate dominant pathology if instability story is weak

## Conservative management priorities
- restore dorsiflexion and ankle-foot mobility where limited
- progressive calf and peroneal strengthening
- static and dynamic balance retraining
- perturbation and sensorimotor retraining
- graded sport / running / cutting / landing exposure
- external support use when helpful during return phases

## Exercise priorities
### Foundation phase
- dorsiflexion mobility
- calf / peroneal strengthening
- single-leg balance on stable surface
- gait and stair confidence

### Progression phase
- unstable-surface and perturbation balance work
- multiplanar strength work
- step-down, hop preparation, deceleration mechanics
- trunk / hip contribution as needed

### Return phase
- hops, cuts, landings, reactive tasks
- uneven-ground and sport-specific exposure
- recurrence-prevention maintenance plan

## When to consider referral
- repeated major sprains despite high-quality rehab
- persistent major instability with failed conservative care
- suspected osteochondral lesion or syndesmosis issue
- major mechanical symptoms or unexplained pain dominance

## Patient education points
- “Instability” usually improves best with rehab, not rest alone.
- Balance and coordination work are treatment, not just extras.
- Bracing may help during return to sport, but it should not replace strength and control rebuilding.
- Previous ankle sprains raise recurrence risk if rehab is cut short.
