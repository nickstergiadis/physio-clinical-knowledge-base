# Lateral Ankle Sprain

## Clinical summary
Lateral ankle sprain (LAS) is among the most common musculoskeletal injuries. Most routine sprains are managed conservatively. Best-practice guidance supports early external support, progressive weight-bearing, restoration of ankle mobility, and progression into exercise-based sensorimotor and strength work rather than prolonged rest.

## Typical presentation
- inversion injury, commonly with plantarflexion
- lateral ankle pain and swelling
- difficulty weight-bearing, walking, stairs, or uneven ground
- bruising may develop over the lateral ankle / foot
- dorsiflexion often becomes restricted

## Differential diagnosis / triage priorities
Consider alternatives or associated injury when there is:
- Ottawa-style fracture concern
- syndesmosis pattern / “high ankle” pain
- osteochondral lesion suspicion
- peroneal tendon subluxation or major tendon injury
- Achilles rupture or posterior heel injury
- neurovascular symptoms

## Subjective findings to prioritize
- exact mechanism
- immediate vs delayed swelling
- weight-bearing ability
- prior ankle sprains
- sense of giving way or instability
- sport / work demands
- previous imaging, braces, or immobilization

## Objective findings
- Ottawa-style fracture screening where relevant
- swelling / bruising / tenderness distribution
- active and passive ROM, especially dorsiflexion
- gait
- stair tolerance
- simple balance once appropriate
- progressive reassessment of functional loading rather than chasing laxity tests too early

## First-line conservative management
- protect with external support matched to severity
- progressively restore weight-bearing
- manage swelling / pain while maintaining movement
- restore dorsiflexion and gait quality
- progress to balance, coordination, and strengthening work
- return to sport/work via graded task exposure

## Exercise priorities
### Early phase
- weight-bearing progression
- pain-limited ankle ROM
- calf / intrinsic activation
- gait normalization
- gentle manual therapy / mobility work as adjunct if useful

### Post-acute phase
- dorsiflexion restoration
- calf strengthening
- peroneal strengthening
- single-leg balance / perturbation work
- step work and controlled landing patterns

### Late phase
- running / cutting / jumping exposure
- sport-specific or work-specific drills
- recurrence prevention plan
- brace/tape decisions for higher-risk return periods

## Patient education points
- A sprain is not just a pain issue; balance and strength deficits commonly persist unless they are retrained.
- Early but protected movement is generally better than prolonged rest.
- Swelling may settle before function fully returns.
- Previous ankle sprain increases reinjury risk, so finishing rehab matters.

## Referral / escalation
Consider referral or further workup when:
- fracture cannot be ruled out clinically
- syndesmosis / osteochondral injury is suspected
- symptoms remain severe or unexpectedly persistent
- there is substantial mechanical locking, marked instability, or recurrent failure to progress
