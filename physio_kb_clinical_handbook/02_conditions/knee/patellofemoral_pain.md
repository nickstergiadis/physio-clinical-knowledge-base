# Patellofemoral Pain

## Clinical summary
Patellofemoral pain (PFP) is a clinical pain syndrome typically characterized by anterior or retropatellar knee pain related to loading of the patellofemoral joint. Current best-practice guidance supports exercise therapy plus education as the primary intervention, with adjuncts selected to match the individual presentation.

## Typical presentation
- anterior or retropatellar knee pain
- pain with squat, stairs, running, hills, jumping, or prolonged sitting
- younger / physically active presentation is common, but not universal
- symptoms often track with training or activity spikes
- swelling is usually absent or minimal
- true locking is not typical

## Subjective findings to prioritize
- recent changes in running / sport / gym / stairs exposure
- pain with squat, descent, hills, prolonged sitting, or repeated knee flexion
- footwear / training surface / recovery changes
- irritability after loading
- previous episodes
- beliefs around maltracking, cartilage wear, and “damage”
- patient goals and preferred activity

## Objective findings
- pain during squat is a useful clinical clue
- step-down tolerance
- single-leg loading control
- quadriceps and hip strength / endurance
- trunk / pelvis control
- ankle dorsiflexion and foot loading strategy where relevant
- confirm absence of dominant competing pathology

## First-line conservative management
- education
- knee-targeted exercise therapy
- consider adding hip-targeted exercise therapy
- progressive re-loading of aggravating tasks
- running / sport modification rather than blanket rest
- dose to irritability, then rebuild capacity

## Adjuncts that may help selected patients
- prefabricated foot orthoses
- patellar taping
- manual therapy as a support, not a stand-alone treatment
- movement retraining
- running retraining
Choice of adjunct should be individualized to needs and preferences following assessment.

## What not to center the plan on
- passive treatment alone
- routine imaging
- surgery for typical PFP
- isolated structural explanations without load / capacity analysis

## Exercise priorities
### Calm-irritable phase
- reduce provocative volume temporarily
- maintain tolerable activity
- low-irritability quadriceps + hip strengthening
- graded squat / step exposure
- symptom-monitoring framework

### Rebuild phase
- progressive knee extensor loading
- hip abductor / extensor strengthening where indicated
- step-down, lunge, split squat, squat progression
- cadence / running or landing modifications if relevant

### Return-to-performance phase
- higher-load strength
- plyometric / speed / deceleration exposure as needed
- sport-specific volume progression
- relapse plan for future training spikes

## When to refer / reconsider
Reassess diagnosis if:
- large effusion, major trauma, true locking, or instability is present
- pain is highly localized to another structure and exam strongly suggests another diagnosis
- symptoms remain refractory despite an adequate, individualized rehab trial

## Patient education points
- PFP is usually managed without imaging or surgery.
- Exercise and education are the main treatment.
- Other supports are chosen only if they fit the individual presentation.
- Load modification should be temporary and strategic, not complete shutdown.
