# Degenerative Meniscal Tear

## Clinical summary
Degenerative meniscal tears are common in middle-aged and older adults and frequently coexist with knee osteoarthritis. Current evidence supports conservative management with exercise-based rehabilitation as first-line care for most degenerative meniscal presentations.

## Typical presentation
- middle-aged or older adult
- joint-line dominant pain or loaded flexion / twist aggravation
- intermittent catching sensations may occur
- symptoms may coexist with OA features
- imaging abnormalities are common and may be incidental

## Key reasoning points
- not every MRI-detected meniscal tear is the main pain generator
- mechanical symptoms do not automatically mean surgery is indicated
- the clinical task is to identify whether this is:
  - degenerative meniscal presentation responding to rehab
  - OA-dominant presentation
  - acute displaced / bucket-handle tear or true locked knee needing surgical review

## Subjective findings to prioritize
- onset: gradual versus twist injury
- swelling timing
- true locking versus painful clicking/catching
- extension block
- loaded rotation / pivot aggravation
- coexistence of classic OA symptoms
- previous imaging and interpretations that may be driving fear

## Objective findings
- gait
- joint-line tenderness
- range of motion, especially extension
- squat / split squat / step-down tolerance
- loaded rotation behavior if appropriate
- effusion
- hip/trunk contribution and lower-limb strength
- avoid over-weighting special tests in isolation

## First-line conservative management
- education about the frequent overlap between degenerative meniscal change and OA
- structured exercise therapy
- progressive lower-limb strengthening
- progressive flexion / rotational tolerance as symptoms allow
- walking / conditioning
- symptom-guided activity modification

## Exercise priorities
### Early phase
- settle irritation
- restore extension
- reduce excessive deconditioning
- sit-to-stand / squat to tolerable depth
- quadriceps and hip strengthening

### Progressive phase
- loaded squat / split squat / step-up
- hamstring and calf strengthening
- controlled rotational tolerance
- gait / walking volume progression

### Late phase
- return to kneeling / twisting / golf / hiking / gym tasks as needed
- relapse and flare planning
- long-term strength maintenance

## Surgical thinking
Surgical review becomes more reasonable when there is:
- true locked knee or persistent major extension block
- severe traumatic displaced tear
- failure of a solid conservative trial with ongoing disabling symptoms in a carefully selected patient

## Patient education points
- MRI changes are common and do not automatically require surgery.
- Exercise therapy is usually first-line care.
- Surgery generally does not show superior long-term outcomes over exercise for most degenerative meniscal tears.
- Mechanical symptoms need to be interpreted carefully; not all “clicking” is surgical.
