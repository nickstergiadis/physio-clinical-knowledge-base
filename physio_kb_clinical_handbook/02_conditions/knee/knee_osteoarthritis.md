# Knee Osteoarthritis

## Clinical summary
Knee osteoarthritis (OA) is a clinical diagnosis in many adults and should usually be managed first with exercise, education, and weight management support when relevant.

## Typical presentation
- age commonly 45+
- activity-related knee pain
- stiffness after rest or on starting movement
- morning stiffness absent or short-lived
- reduced tolerance for walking, stairs, sit-to-stand, kneeling, or longer standing
- function often becomes limited before strength fully recovers

## Diagnosis
### Clinical diagnosis is often enough
A straightforward OA presentation is commonly diagnosed clinically without routine imaging when there are no atypical features.

### Imaging
Consider imaging when:
- the presentation is atypical
- another diagnosis is reasonably suspected
- surgical planning is being considered
- symptoms are disproportionately severe or unexplained

## Subjective findings to prioritize
- pain behavior with walking, stairs, hills, sit-to-stand, and kneeling
- 24-hour stiffness pattern
- flare triggers and flare duration
- swelling history
- current activity level and deconditioning
- bodyweight and cardiometabolic context when relevant
- prior injections / medications / braces / surgery
- confidence, fear, and expectation around “wear and tear”

## Objective findings
- gait and walking tolerance
- knee extension and flexion
- effusion / swelling
- sit-to-stand and squat tolerance
- step-up / step-down tolerance
- quadriceps strength
- hip strength / single-leg control
- balance and general lower-limb capacity

## First-line conservative management
- education that OA is usually managed clinically and actively
- therapeutic exercise tailored to needs
- supervised exercise when useful
- encourage persistence through early symptom flares from exercise
- combine exercise with education / behaviour change support where appropriate
- weight-loss support when living with overweight or obesity
- walking aids if needed
- selective device use only when instability / biomechanical loading issues justify it

## Exercise priorities
### Early phase
- regain extension and comfortable flexion
- frequent movement exposure
- sit-to-stand practice
- walking dosage matched to irritability
- simple quadriceps, hamstring, calf, and hip strengthening

### Progressive phase
- heavier quadriceps and lower-limb strengthening
- stair/step exposure
- squat and hinge patterns
- balance / proprioception
- aerobic conditioning
- self-management of flare-ups

### Longer-term phase
- capacity building for valued activities
- relapse planning
- ongoing strength and aerobic work
- de-medicalize fear around “bone on bone” while staying honest about symptom variability

## Adjuncts / medications
- topical NSAIDs are first-line drug therapy for knee OA when medication is needed
- oral NSAIDs may be considered if topical medication is ineffective or unsuitable and risk profile permits
- intra-articular corticosteroid injection may be considered for short-term relief or to support participation in exercise
- do not routinely offer paracetamol or weak opioids as core treatment
- do not offer strong opioids or glucosamine
- do not offer hyaluronan injections
- manual therapy, if used, should be alongside exercise rather than instead of it
- do not offer acupuncture/dry needling or routine electrotherapy for OA management

## When to refer
Consider referral when:
- diagnosis is uncertain
- substantial symptoms persist despite adequate conservative care
- end-stage OA is likely and quality of life remains significantly limited
- the person wishes to discuss surgical options after an appropriate non-surgical trial

## Patient education points
- OA is usually diagnosed clinically.
- Imaging is not needed for every case.
- Exercise is treatment, not just an add-on.
- Pain may rise temporarily when beginning exercise; long-term adherence matters.
- Weight loss can meaningfully improve pain and function if relevant.
