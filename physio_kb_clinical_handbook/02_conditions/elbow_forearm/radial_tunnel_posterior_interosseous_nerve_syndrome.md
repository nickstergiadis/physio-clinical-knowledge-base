# Radial Tunnel Syndrome / Posterior Interosseous Nerve Syndrome

## Clinical summary
These are radial nerve entrapment presentations around the proximal forearm / supinator region. In practice, separate the predominantly pain-dominant radial tunnel picture from the more motor-dominant posterior interosseous nerve (PIN) syndrome.

## Typical presentation
### Radial tunnel syndrome
- Lateral proximal forearm pain, often distal to the lateral epicondyle
- Pain with gripping, lifting, resisted supination, or repeated forearm use
- Often confused with lateral elbow tendinopathy
- Sensory loss is usually absent or minimal

### PIN syndrome
- Motor weakness of finger / thumb extension or wrist extension pattern changes
- Forearm ache may or may not be present
- Clear sensory loss is not the dominant feature because PIN is mainly motor

## Subjective findings to prioritize
- Exact location of pain: epicondyle vs 3–5 cm distal in radial tunnel region
- Pain with supination, gripping, lifting, keyboard/mouse, screwdriver/tool use
- Any finger or thumb extension weakness
- History of repetitive pronation-supination, trauma, surgery, or mass effect
- Whether the presentation behaves like tendon overload, nerve irritability, or mixed

## Objective findings
- Lateral elbow tenderness map and comparison with common extensor origin
- Resisted long-finger extension / resisted supination symptom behavior where relevant
- Finger / thumb / wrist extensor strength screen
- Cervical and proximal radial nerve differential screen
- Rule out lateral elbow tendinopathy, PIN palsy, and referred pain

## Differential diagnosis
- Lateral elbow tendinopathy
- Cervical radiculopathy
- PIN syndrome
- Posterolateral elbow instability
- Intra-articular elbow pathology
- Supinator / extensor compartment overload

## First-line conservative management
- Education and load modification
- Reduce repetitive gripping + pronation/supination irritation initially
- Progressive forearm / grip / upper-quarter strengthening as symptoms allow
- Nerve mobility only if it clearly helps and does not flare symptoms
- Address workstation, tool use, lifting mechanics, and sport-specific exposure

## Practical distinctions that matter clinically
- **Lateral elbow tendinopathy:** pain usually at or near common extensor origin; tendon load testing predominates
- **Radial tunnel syndrome:** pain often slightly more distal; provoked by radial tunnel stressors; neural features may still be subtle
- **PIN syndrome:** motor deficit is the key escalation feature

## When to refer
Consider medical / specialist review when:
- objective motor deficit is present
- weakness is progressive
- symptoms are severe or atypical
- mass lesion / space-occupying process is suspected
- rehab for an apparently non-operative case is not changing pain or function
