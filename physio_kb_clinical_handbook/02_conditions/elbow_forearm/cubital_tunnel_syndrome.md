# Cubital Tunnel Syndrome (Ulnar Neuropathy at the Elbow)

## Clinical summary
Cubital tunnel syndrome is an ulnar nerve entrapment / irritation presentation at the elbow, usually involving compression, traction, or repeated irritation around the cubital tunnel or retrocondylar groove.

## Typical presentation
- Medial elbow / proximal forearm ache or irritability
- Paresthesia or numbness in the ring and little fingers
- Symptoms with prolonged elbow flexion, leaning on the elbow, sleeping with flexed elbows, phone use, desk work, cycling, or repeated throwing / lifting
- Reduced grip, clumsiness, hand intrinsic fatigue, or dropping objects in more progressed cases
- May coexist with medial elbow overload pain

## Subjective findings to prioritize
- Distribution of sensory symptoms
- Night symptoms or waking with numbness
- Symptom behavior with elbow flexion and pressure on the tunnel
- Workstation, driving, phone, gym, or sport exposures
- Change in dexterity, grip, pinch, or hand endurance
- Neck / thoracic outlet / wrist history to screen for double-crush or alternate sources

## Objective findings
- Sensory change in the ulnar distribution
- Reproduction with sustained elbow flexion / local provocation where relevant
- Grip and key-pinch comparison
- Hand intrinsic performance: interossei / first dorsal interosseous / adductor pollicis patterns
- Cervical and peripheral nerve differential screen
- Observe for more advanced intrinsic wasting or clawing only if severity is higher

## Differential diagnosis
- Medial elbow tendinopathy
- Cervical radiculopathy
- Thoracic outlet / brachial plexus irritability
- Guyon canal syndrome
- Generalized peripheral neuropathy
- Double-crush presentation

## First-line conservative management
- Education and activity modification
- Reduce sustained elbow flexion and external pressure on the tunnel
- Night positioning or splinting if flexion-related night symptoms are prominent
- Workstation / phone / cycling / lifting modification
- Graded nerve mobility work only if clearly tolerated
- Progressive load for the upper quarter and hand as symptoms settle
- Manual therapy only as an adjunct, not as the main plan

## Practical treatment priorities
### Early / irritable
- unload flexion-pressure triggers
- reduce sleep provocation
- symptom monitoring
- non-provocative hand / wrist / forearm / scapular loading

### Mid stage
- gradual return to daily elbow flexion exposure
- progressive grip / forearm / upper-quarter strengthening
- task-specific endurance work

### Late stage
- return to gym / sport / work tolerance
- restore repetitive-load capacity
- ensure symptom stability with full-day exposure

## When to refer
Consider medical / surgical review when:
- progressive weakness is present
- persistent sensory loss or motor deficit is developing
- intrinsic wasting is evident
- symptoms fail to improve despite a good conservative trial
- diagnosis is uncertain or another neurologic process is suspected
