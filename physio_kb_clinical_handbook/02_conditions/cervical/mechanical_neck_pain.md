# Mechanical / Non-Specific Neck Pain

## Clinical summary
Mechanical neck pain is a broad clinical label for neck-dominant pain where symptoms are meaningfully modified by posture, movement, loading, or activity and no serious pathology pattern is evident. Most cases are managed conservatively.

## Typical presentation
- neck pain and stiffness
- pain with sustained desk posture, driving, looking down, or turning
- referral to upper trapezius, scapular region, or proximal arm may occur
- headache may coexist
- symptoms often fluctuate with activity and irritability

## Subjective findings to prioritize
- trauma vs gradual onset
- headache or arm symptoms
- sleep disturbance and 24-hour pattern
- work setup and sustained posture exposure
- training / gym / lifting exposure
- previous episodes and beliefs about “disc,” “pinched nerve,” or posture damage

## Objective findings
- active ROM and symptom response
- repeated movement / sustained posture response
- upper-quarter neuro screen if arm symptoms present
- thoracic mobility and scapular contribution
- deep neck flexor / extensor endurance where relevant
- palpation only as a supporting finding, not the diagnosis

## First-line conservative management
- reassurance and education
- progressive exercise therapy
- symptom-guided exposure to movement
- thoracic / scapular adjunct work when relevant
- manual therapy as a short-term adjunct, not the whole plan
- ergonomic changes only where they clearly matter

## Exercise priorities
### Calm-irritable phase
- comfortable ROM and unloading strategies
- low-load deep flexor work if tolerated
- thoracic mobility / breathing / scapular support work
- walking and general activity

### Rebuild phase
- cervical strength-endurance
- scapular / thoracic contribution
- graded tolerance to rotation, extension, and sustained positions
- lifting / work exposure rebuilding

### Return-to-demand phase
- heavier carries, pulling, pressing as appropriate
- sport or work-specific repeated loading
- self-management plan for flare-ups

## When to refer / reconsider
Reassess if:
- symptoms are non-mechanical, progressive, or neurologically worsening
- headache or dizziness suggests a non-MSK driver
- marked trauma changes the picture
- a solid rehab trial fails and diagnosis needs reframing

## Patient education points
- Neck pain is common and usually improves without invasive care.
- Pain with movement does not automatically mean harm.
- Long-term recovery usually depends more on graded loading than passive treatment.
