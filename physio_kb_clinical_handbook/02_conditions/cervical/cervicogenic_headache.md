# Cervicogenic Headache

## Diagnosis
- unilateral or side-dominant headache associated with neck pain/stiffness
- headache reproduced by neck movement, sustained posture, or cervical palpation/loading
- reduced upper-cervical mobility is common
- diagnose as a **secondary headache** after screening for primary headache and vascular/neurologic red flags

## Clinical classification
- **mobility-dominant upper-cervical pattern**
- **movement-control/endurance dominant pattern**
- **mixed cervicogenic + primary headache overlap** (common in clinic)

## Differential diagnosis and red flags
Differentiate from:
- migraine, tension-type headache, cluster headache
- occipital neuralgia
- cervicogenic dizziness pattern vs vestibular causes

Urgent referral triggers:
- sudden severe “thunderclap” onset
- new focal neurologic deficits, dysarthria, diplopia, ataxia, drop attacks
- suspected cervical arterial dysfunction pattern
- headache with fever, meningism, cancer history, or unexplained systemic illness
- new progressive headache pattern in older adults

## Subjective exam priorities
- headache pattern: onset, frequency, duration, side dominance
- neck movement/posture provocation and easing factors
- associated migraine features (photophobia, nausea, aura)
- trauma history, especially recent whiplash
- medication-overuse risk and analgesic frequency
- sleep, stress, and work-position contributors

## Objective exam priorities
- active cervical ROM, especially rotation/extension provocation
- flexion-rotation test for upper-cervical contribution
- segmental palpation/loading response (C0–C3 region)
- deep neck flexor endurance and scapular control
- neurological and cranial nerve screen if atypical features present

## Useful tests and limits
- **Flexion-rotation test:** supports upper-cervical dysfunction; not specific for headache type alone
- **Manual examination/provocation:** may reproduce familiar pain; examiner-dependent reliability
- **Headache diaries + disability scales:** useful for monitoring trend, not diagnosis on their own

## Management
- education on secondary headache mechanisms and trigger control
- graded neck mobility + endurance loading
- short-term manual therapy as adjunct to exercise
- headache self-management plan (early flare action, pacing, sleep/stress hygiene)
- coordinate with medical provider when primary headache overlap or medication strategy needs review

## Exercise progression logic
1. low-irritability cervical mobility and symptom-calming drills
2. deep neck flexor/extensor endurance and scapulothoracic support
3. functional postural/load tolerance for work/screen/sport exposures
4. relapse-prevention program with self-dosed flare management

## Escalation / imaging / procedures
- image or refer when pattern is atypical, progressive, trauma-linked, or neurologically concerning
- consider neurology/headache specialist input when primary headache overlap dominates or response stalls
- injections are not first-line in routine cervicogenic headache rehab
