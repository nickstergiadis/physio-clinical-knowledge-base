# Cervicogenic Headache

## Clinical summary
Cervicogenic headache is headache referred from cervical structures, commonly with accompanying neck pain, reduced cervical movement, and provocation by neck posture or movement. Diagnosis is clinical and requires exclusion of competing headache disorders.

## Typical presentation
- unilateral or side-dominant headache is common
- headache associated with neck pain / stiffness
- pain may begin in suboccipital or upper cervical region and spread anteriorly
- neck movement, sustained posture, or upper cervical loading may provoke symptoms
- photophobia / nausea are usually less dominant than in migraine, though overlap can occur

## Subjective findings to prioritize
- headache location and spread
- relationship between neck pain and headache
- aggravating neck postures / movements
- trauma history
- migraine features and medical headache red flags
- frequency, irritability, and work / screen exposure

## Objective findings
- cervical ROM and symptom provocation
- upper cervical movement / manual exam where appropriate
- flexion-rotation style limitations may support the pattern
- neck muscle performance and endurance
- thoracic / scapular contributors
- neuro screen where needed to exclude competing pathology

## Conservative management
- education on headache triggers and pacing
- cervical and upper-quarter exercise
- manual therapy as an adjunct in selected patients
- progressive self-management rather than dependence on passive care
- sleep, stress, and work-exposure review

## Exercise priorities
- upper cervical and lower cervical movement tolerance
- deep neck flexor performance
- scapular / thoracic support
- headache-trigger exposure reduction without avoidance
- gradual return to reading, screens, driving, gym, and overhead work as relevant

## What to be careful with
- do not overconfidently label all unilateral headache as cervicogenic
- screen for migraine, vascular, neurological, and systemic headache red flags
- use combined findings, not one test in isolation

## Patient education points
- Cervicogenic headache is usually treated with conservative care.
- Short-term symptom relief is useful, but lasting improvement usually needs exercise and exposure change.
- Headache and neck pain often improve together when the cervical source is addressed.
