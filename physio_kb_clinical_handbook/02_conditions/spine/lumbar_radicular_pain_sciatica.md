# Lumbar Radicular Pain / Sciatica

## Clinical summary
Lumbar radicular pain (commonly labelled sciatica when it follows the sciatic distribution) is leg pain arising from irritation or inflammation of a lumbar nerve root. It may occur with or without low back pain and may or may not be accompanied by neurological deficit.

## Typical presentation
- buttock and leg pain, often more clinically important than back pain
- possible below-knee symptoms
- sharp, shooting, burning, electric, or searing quality
- numbness, tingling, altered sensation, or weakness may occur
- sitting, cough / sneeze, neural loading, or certain lumbar movements may aggravate symptoms

## Subjective findings to prioritize
- exact symptom distribution and whether pain travels below the knee
- neural symptom descriptors: burning, electric, numbness, tingling
- severity and irritability
- cough / sneeze / strain effect
- sitting tolerance and night pain
- any progressive weakness, gait change, foot drop, bladder / bowel change, saddle symptoms
- prior imaging and whether it matches the current clinical picture

## Objective findings
- neurological screen: myotomes, dermatomes, reflexes as indicated
- neural mechanosensitivity tests where appropriate
- repeated movement / directional response if helpful
- gait and functional deficit screen
- rule out hip and other peripheral neural contributors

## Differential diagnosis
- non-specific lumbar-related leg pain
- lumbar spinal stenosis / neurogenic claudication
- peripheral nerve entrapment
- hip pathology referring into the thigh
- vascular claudication
- serious pathology including cauda equina, tumour, infection

## First-line conservative management
- education on prognosis, warning signs, and staying appropriately active
- avoid prolonged bed rest
- graded movement and exercise matched to irritability
- walking / positional strategies that reduce leg symptoms
- progressive trunk and lower-quarter exercise as symptoms allow
- manual therapy only as an adjunct within an active plan
- medication decisions should be cautious and evidence-aware

## Practical exercise priorities
### Acute / highly irritable
- find positions / movements that reduce distal symptoms
- gentle repeated movements or unloading strategies if helpful
- short, frequent walking exposure where tolerated
- avoid forcing high-irritability neural provocation early

### Improving phase
- restore lumbar and lower-quarter movement confidence
- trunk endurance and lower-limb strengthening
- graded neural loading only when symptoms and irritability allow
- progress sitting, bending, and lifting tolerance gradually

### Persistent phase
- strength, endurance, and aerobic rebuilding
- graded return to full work / sport demands
- persistent-pain and psychosocial drivers addressed if recovery stalls

## What tends to help most
- reassurance plus monitoring for neurological progression
- active management
- graded exercise
- time and natural recovery in many cases
- targeted escalation only when clinically indicated

## What to avoid over-relying on
- routine imaging in non-specialist settings
- gabapentinoids, oral steroids, benzodiazepines, or chronic opioids as standard sciatica treatment
- traction as routine care
- complete rest

## Medication notes
- NICE advises against gabapentinoids, other antiepileptics, oral corticosteroids, and benzodiazepines for sciatica because overall benefit is not shown and harm exists.
- NICE advises against opioids for chronic sciatica.
- If NSAIDs are used, use risk assessment and the lowest effective dose for the shortest practical period.

## When to refer
Urgent referral if:
- cauda equina features
- progressive or severe neurological deficit
- major unexplained weakness / foot drop

Non-urgent specialist opinion may be appropriate when:
- severe acute sciatica persists
- symptoms remain meaningfully limiting despite a good conservative trial
- imaging is likely to change management in a specialist pathway
- epidural or surgical options are being considered

## Invasive / surgical considerations
- NICE allows consideration of epidural local anaesthetic plus steroid for acute and severe sciatica.
- NICE allows consideration of spinal decompression when non-surgical treatment has not improved pain or function and imaging findings are consistent with sciatic symptoms.

## Patient education points
- Leg symptoms often matter more than the scan.
- Many cases improve without surgery, but worsening neurological findings need rapid review.
- The aim is to reduce leg symptom severity, restore function, and monitor nerve-related change over time.
