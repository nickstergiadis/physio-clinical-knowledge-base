# Non-specific Low Back Pain

## Clinical summary
Non-specific low back pain refers to lumbar pain that cannot be confidently explained by a single specific pathoanatomical diagnosis such as fracture, infection, malignancy, inflammatory disease, cauda equina syndrome, or a clearly dominant radicular syndrome.

## Typical presentation
- axial lumbar / lumbosacral pain, stiffness, or movement-related pain
- may refer to buttock or proximal thigh without a clear nerve-root pattern
- aggravated by bending, lifting, prolonged sitting, standing, or transitions
- fluctuating severity and irritability are common
- high disability does not require severe tissue damage

## Subjective findings to prioritize
- dominant pain location and behaviour
- aggravating / easing factors
- 24-hour pattern and sleep disruption
- work and sitting / standing / walking tolerance
- beliefs, fear, distress, and recovery expectations
- previous episodes and prior response to care
- red flags and non-MSK screening

## Objective findings
- baseline movement tolerance: flexion, extension, side bend, sit-to-stand, gait
- repeated movement / sustained loading response where clinically useful
- functional tasks: squat, hinge, step, carry, transfers
- hip contribution screen
- neurological screen if symptoms extend into the leg or history suggests it
- palpation only as a low-priority adjunct

## Differential diagnosis
- lumbar radicular pain / sciatica
- hip OA or intra-articular hip pathology
- SIJ-related presentation
- vertebral fracture
- inflammatory spinal pain
- malignancy / infection
- renal / abdominal / pelvic referral

## First-line conservative management
- clear explanation and reassurance
- advice to continue or resume normal activity as tolerated
- symptom-calming movement exposure rather than rest
- structured exercise and graded activity progression
- targeted trunk / hip / whole-body strengthening and endurance work
- walking / aerobic reconditioning where appropriate
- psychological-informed care when fear, distress, or avoidance are prominent
- manual therapy only as an adjunct within an active plan

## Practical exercise priorities
### Acute / irritable flare
- frequent tolerable movement breaks
- unloaded or low-load movement that settles symptoms
- walking as tolerated
- simple trunk / hip activation and control drills
- breathing / down-regulation if guarded and distressed

### Subacute / recovering
- rebuild movement confidence
- progress hinging, squatting, carrying, sit-to-stand capacity
- add trunk endurance, hip strengthening, and walking volume
- graded return to work and lifting exposure

### Persistent / recurrent
- progressive strength and endurance loading
- aerobic capacity
- movement variability rather than rigid posture rules
- address psychosocial barriers and self-management skills
- relapse planning

## What tends to help most
- education and reassurance
- staying active
- structured exercise
- graded exposure to valued function
- multimodal care when psychosocial obstacles are relevant

## What to avoid over-relying on
- routine imaging without indication
- bed rest and excessive protection
- passive treatment as the whole plan
- traction, belts/corsets, and routine electrotherapies
- opioids for chronic low back pain
- paracetamol alone as a core strategy

## Medication notes
- NSAIDs may be considered if appropriate risk assessment supports use.
- Use the lowest effective dose for the shortest practical period.
- Chronic opioid use is not recommended as routine management.

## When to refer
Consider escalation / referral when:
- red flags or alternative pathology are suspected
- serious or progressive neurological findings appear
- symptoms remain severe and functionally limiting despite an adequate rehab trial
- diagnostic uncertainty remains high
- invasive options are being considered for a defined subgroup (e.g. suspected facet-mediated chronic pain pathway)

## Patient education points
- Most low back pain is not explained by one dangerous structural lesion.
- Imaging often shows age-related findings that are common even in people without pain.
- Improvement is usually helped by guided activity, not by waiting for pain to disappear before moving.
- Recurrence risk can be managed by capacity building and early flare management.
