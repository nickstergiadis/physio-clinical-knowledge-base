# Thumb CMC Osteoarthritis

## Clinical summary
Thumb CMC osteoarthritis is a common cause of thumb-base pain, pinch weakness, and hand function loss. Diagnosis is usually clinical when the symptom pattern is typical, and first-line care remains exercise, education, and selective orthotic support.

## Typical presentation
- pain at the thumb base with pinch, grip, opening jars, key use, texting, or lifting
- stiffness after inactivity or after heavier hand use
- reduced pinch strength and hand endurance
- symptoms often fluctuate with repetitive load or prolonged caregiving/manual tasks

## Subjective findings to prioritize
- exact pain map at the thumb base vs radial styloid or volar wrist
- pinch and grip task irritability
- morning stiffness and flare duration
- dominant hand demands, caregiving, device use, and tool use
- prior splinting, injection, or imaging history
- screen for inflammatory arthropathy, traumatic injury, or neural symptoms when the picture is atypical

## Objective findings
- local tenderness and symptom reproduction at the first CMC joint
- compare pinch, grip, and thumb opposition capacity
- observe functional tasks such as key pinch, jar opening, lifting, and writing
- screen de Quervain, fracture, and nerve entrapment patterns when indicated
- assess whether limitation is primarily pain-dominant, stiffness-dominant, or capacity-deficit dominant

## Differential diagnosis and red flags
Differentiate from:
- de Quervain tenosynovitis
- scaphoid or trapezial fracture after trauma
- inflammatory arthropathy
- broader hand/wrist tendinopathy or peripheral nerve entrapment

Escalate or refer when:
- traumatic onset suggests fracture/dislocation
- swelling, heat, or multi-joint involvement raises inflammatory or infectious concern
- rapid functional decline or severe persistent disability is not responding to an appropriate conservative plan

## First-line management
- education that OA symptoms can often be improved without surgery
- thumb CMC orthosis or taping when it meaningfully improves task tolerance
- progressive thumb, pinch, grip, and forearm strengthening within irritability limits
- activity modification that reduces unnecessary flare triggers without complete unloading
- pair pain management with functional goals such as lifting, opening containers, typing, or infant care

## Exercise progression
- begin with symptom-calming ROM and low-load isometric opposition/abduction work
- progress to tolerated pinch and grip strengthening
- build hand endurance and task-specific loading for work, caregiving, and ADLs
- keep the dose flare-aware rather than alternating overuse and full rest

## Referral / imaging / injections / surgery
- imaging is usually reserved for trauma, atypical presentation, or surgical planning
- injection pathways may be considered when pain remains high and blocks functional rehab
- refer for hand-surgery discussion when pain and loss of function remain substantial despite a well-executed conservative trial
