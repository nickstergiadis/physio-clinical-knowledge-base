# Traumatic Anterior Shoulder Instability

## Clinical summary
Traumatic anterior shoulder instability is a spectrum that includes first-time dislocation, recurrent dislocation, and symptomatic subluxation / apprehension states. Assessment and management should be shaped by age, recurrence status, activity level, sport, bone loss concerns, associated soft-tissue injury, and recurrence risk.

## Typical presentation
- traumatic mechanism
- clear dislocation / subluxation history or apprehension after trauma
- apprehension in abducted / externally rotated positions
- recurrent “slipping,” “dead arm,” or instability episodes in some patients
- younger active / collision-sport patients often have higher recurrence risk
- older adults may present with more associated cuff injury or soft-tissue damage

## Subjective findings to prioritize
- exact mechanism of injury
- whether reduction was required
- first-time vs recurrent event
- occupation / sport / collision exposure
- hyperlaxity history
- prior surgery
- patient goals and tolerance for recurrence risk
- confidence and psychological readiness

## Objective findings
- protect the exam in the acute phase
- neurovascular screen after dislocation
- apprehension / relocation style testing when appropriate
- generalized laxity / hypermobility context
- cuff and scapular control
- assess for associated cuff injury, especially in older adults
- imaging decisions should be linked to age, recurrence, and suspicion of bone loss / structural injury

## Conservative management themes
- appropriate initial protection after reduction / acute event
- progressive ROM within safe limits
- dynamic stabilizer strengthening
- scapular, cuff, and kinetic-chain control
- criteria-based progression rather than time alone
- return-to-sport testing and psychological readiness matter

## When early surgical discussion is more likely
- recurrent instability
- high-risk recurrence profile
- significant glenoid or humeral bone loss concern
- high-demand collision / contact athlete
- failure of a meaningful nonoperative trial
- structural context where recurrence risk is unacceptably high for patient goals

## Rehab priorities
### Acute / protected phase
- pain control
- edema management
- safe ROM introduction
- maintain distal function
- educate on recurrence risk and activity restrictions

### Control-building phase
- cuff co-contraction / dynamic stabilization
- scapular control
- progressive closed- and open-chain loading
- regain confidence in mid-range and then outer-range positions

### Advanced function / return-to-sport phase
- outer-range control
- perturbation and deceleration work
- contact / collision / overhead specific exposure where relevant
- criteria-based RTS testing

## What not to miss
- associated greater tuberosity fracture
- nerve injury
- cuff tear in older patient
- significant bone loss / recurrent instability pathway
- persistent apprehension despite pain improvement

## Patient education points
- Not all instability cases are the same; age, sport, and recurrence history matter.
- Rehab must address both confidence and dynamic control.
- Surgical referral is more likely in some high-recurrence-risk groups.
- Return to sport should be criteria-based, not purely calendar-based.
