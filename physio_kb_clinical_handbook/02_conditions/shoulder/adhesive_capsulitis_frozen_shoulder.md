# Adhesive Capsulitis / Frozen Shoulder

## Clinical summary
Adhesive capsulitis is a clinical syndrome of painful shoulder stiffness characterized by substantial loss of passive motion, especially external rotation. It is commonly self-limiting but often prolonged, so treatment aims to improve pain and function as efficiently as possible and align care with patient goals and irritability.

## Typical presentation
- gradual onset shoulder pain and stiffness
- marked passive restriction in multiple planes
- external rotation commonly most limited
- dressing, grooming, reaching overhead, and hand-behind-back are restricted
- symptoms often last months rather than days / weeks
- pain may settle while stiffness remains prominent

## Risk factors / associations
- diabetes mellitus
- thyroid disease / endocrine associations
- age commonly 40-65
- female sex more common
- previous frozen shoulder / possible contralateral history

## Subjective findings to prioritize
- slow onset vs post-traumatic / post-operative onset
- stage-like narrative (painful / freezing / stiffening / recovery), while recognizing overlap
- night pain and sleep loss
- functional activities most limited by stiffness
- diabetes / thyroid history
- previous injection / physio / hydrodilatation / surgery history

## Objective findings
- loss of passive external rotation is the key clue
- passive loss usually exceeds what would be expected from pain alone
- active ROM generally mirrors passive restriction
- cuff strength may appear reduced secondary to pain / ROM restriction
- rule out major glenohumeral OA, fracture/dislocation history, and other competing causes
- imaging is not usually required to confirm the diagnosis, but plain radiographs may be used when the presentation is atypical or to exclude other pathology

## First-line conservative management
- education on condition course and shared decision-making
- pain-guided mobility and function work
- home exercise program matched to irritability
- in-person physiotherapy where useful for coaching and progression
- consider glenohumeral corticosteroid injection / hydrodilatation discussions with medical colleagues where consistent with local practice and stage / irritability
- avoid aggressive stretching that creates prolonged flare when the shoulder is highly irritable

## Rehab priorities
### Pain-dominant / early-irritable phase
- settle symptoms enough to preserve movement and sleep
- gentle ROM in tolerable range
- education about pacing and expected course
- consider medical adjuncts if pain blocks participation

### Stiffness-dominant phase
- progressive mobility work
- functional reaching exposure
- graded strengthening in available range
- scapular and trunk strategies to regain access without overcompensating

### Recovery / thawing phase
- continue ROM restoration
- rebuild strength and endurance
- restore overhead and behind-the-back function
- relapse-prevention and expectation setting

## When to refer / reconsider
Reconsider the working diagnosis if:
- restriction pattern is not convincing
- trauma, fracture, instability, or red flags are present
- radiographic OA better explains the presentation
- severe weakness suggests major cuff tear
- progress is absent and another diagnosis becomes more likely

## Patient education points
- Frozen shoulder is usually diagnosed clinically.
- It often takes months, and sometimes longer, to recover.
- The goal is not just “more stretch”; it is better pain control, function, and staged mobility recovery.
- Shared decision-making matters because acceptable symptom relief and speed of recovery vary by patient.
