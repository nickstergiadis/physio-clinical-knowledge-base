# Rotator Cuff-Related Shoulder Pain Management — Evidence Summary

## Clinical question
In adults with non-traumatic rotator cuff-related shoulder pain (RCRSP), what management strategy produces the best short- to medium-term outcomes for pain, function, and return to activity in outpatient MSK practice?

## Key findings
- **Exercise-centered care remains first-line.** Recent shoulder guidance and synthesis papers consistently prioritize education + progressive loading as the default base plan.
- **There is no single “best exercise.”** Better outcomes appear linked to dosing quality, progression logic, and adherence rather than one named protocol.
- **Imaging is usually not first-line in straightforward non-traumatic cases.** Imaging is more useful when traumatic tear suspicion, red flags, or non-response to a robust rehab trial changes management decisions.
- **Adjuncts can help symptoms, but should not displace loading progression.** Manual therapy and pharmacologic pathways may improve short-term participation, yet active progression is the central long-term driver.
- **Clinical reasoning should use pattern-based exam clusters.** Diagnostic confidence is stronger with history + exam behavior + response-to-load trends versus isolated special-test positives.

## Study/guideline quality signal
- **Stronger signal:** contemporary clinical practice guideline and systematic review/meta-analysis level sources support conservative-first exercise-based care.
- **Moderate signal:** dose details, subgrouping precision, and intervention sequencing vary across studies and are less definitive.
- **Lower-certainty zones:** highly specific exercise parameter superiority claims and long-term subgroup prediction remain less settled.

## Clinical bottom line
Use a **conservative-first, exercise-led pathway** for most non-traumatic RCRSP presentations: patient education, strategic load modification (not prolonged rest), progressive cuff/scapular/functional loading, and objective reassessment every 2-4 weeks. Escalate for imaging/specialist review when trauma, major weakness, red flags, or non-response after an adequate rehabilitation dose suggests management should change.

## Applicability / caveats
- Best fit: outpatient adults with mechanical, load-sensitive shoulder pain and preserved/near-preserved passive ROM.
- Adjust expectations for higher irritability, significant deconditioning, metabolic comorbidity, or prolonged symptom duration.
- Early flare-ups can occur during reloading; progression should be tied to 24-hour response and function trend, not pain-free perfection.
- Overhead athletes and high-demand workers may need earlier integration of endurance, velocity, and task-specific exposure layers.

## Controversies / limitations
- Terminology and subgroup boundaries still vary (RCRSP vs subacromial pain syndrome vs cuff tendinopathy framing).
- Comparative superiority between exercise styles is inconsistent across trials.
- Injection sequencing and ideal combined-care timing remain debated and patient-specific.
- Evidence quality is stronger for broad management principles than for narrow protocol “winners.”
