# Shoulder Clinical Reasoning

## Goal
Separate common outpatient shoulder presentations into useful management pathways:
- rotator cuff-related shoulder pain / rotator cuff tendinopathy
- adhesive capsulitis / frozen shoulder
- traumatic anterior shoulder instability

## Step 1 — Decide which dominant pattern is present
### Pain-dominant / load-sensitive pattern
Think:
- rotator cuff-related shoulder pain
- bursal / tendon-dominant irritation
- referred pain if exam does not fit local shoulder loading

Common clues:
- pain with elevation, reaching, lifting, pressing, throwing
- painful arc / resisted loading sensitivity
- passive ROM often near preserved or only mildly limited by pain

### Stiffness-dominant pattern
Think:
- adhesive capsulitis / frozen shoulder
- post-immobilization stiffness
- osteoarthritis if age / imaging / crepitus pattern fits

Common clues:
- marked passive restriction in multiple planes
- external rotation often most limited
- dressing, reaching behind back, grooming strongly limited
- symptoms often persist >3 months

### Instability-dominant pattern
Think:
- traumatic anterior shoulder instability
- recurrent subluxation / apprehension
- labral / capsuloligamentous injury context

Common clues:
- trauma history
- apprehension rather than just pain in abduction-external rotation
- “dead arm,” slipping, subluxation, or recurrent episodes
- younger athlete / collision sport / high-demand overhead profile

## Step 2 — Exclude competing causes
Reconsider the working diagnosis if you find:
- marked neck-related symptom reproduction
- neurological deficit
- major traumatic cuff tear suspicion
- systemic inflammatory or infective features
- fracture suspicion
- tumor / non-mechanical pain pattern
- AC joint dominant focal pain
- true locking or significant traumatic weakness

## Step 3 — Decide which exam findings actually change management
Helpful findings:
- passive ROM pattern
- active ROM tolerance
- resisted cuff loading response
- apprehension / relocation pattern where appropriate
- scapular and trunk control during relevant tasks
- symptom behavior after loading

Less useful:
- over-interpreting single orthopedic tests as structure-specific truth
- assuming imaging equals the pain source
- centering the plan on posture alone

## Step 4 — Match treatment to the dominant problem
### If pain-dominant / RCRSP
- education
- progressive loading
- activity modification, not full shutdown
- adjuncts only if they help engagement and function

### If stiffness-dominant / adhesive capsulitis
- education on expected course
- staged pain relief + mobility work
- consider medical co-management / injection discussions where appropriate
- push-forceful stretching with caution

### If instability-dominant
- classify first-time vs recurrent
- consider age, sport, collision exposure, bone loss concerns, and recurrence risk
- load dynamic stabilizers and control in meaningful positions
- refer early when recurrence risk or structural injury profile is high

## Red flags / urgent referral triggers
- suspected fracture or unreduced dislocation
- acute traumatic inability to actively elevate with major weakness
- infection signs
- tumor suspicion / constant unremitting pain
- progressive neurological findings
- vascular compromise
