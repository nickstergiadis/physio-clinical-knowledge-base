# Lumbar Anatomy and Biomechanics

## Purpose of this page
Use this page as the stable background layer for lumbar condition pages. It should change slowly compared with treatment recommendations.

## Region summary
The lumbar region links the thorax, pelvis, and lower limbs. It must balance:
- load transfer
- movement variability
- segmental control
- tolerance to repeated flexion, extension, rotation, and compression

## Key structures
- 5 lumbar vertebrae in most adults
- intervertebral discs
- facet (zygapophyseal) joints
- vertebral endplates
- ligaments: ALL, PLL, ligamentum flavum, interspinous, supraspinous
- paraspinals / multifidus
- abdominal wall / trunk musculature
- thoracolumbar fascia
- sacroiliac region as an adjacent contributor
- lumbar nerve roots and dural / neural structures

## Functional themes
### 1. Load sharing
Lumbar load is shared across:
- discs
- facet joints
- passive ligamentous structures
- active muscular support
- pressure and force transmission through the thoracolumbar / abdominal canister

### 2. Movement is normal; overload is context-dependent
Pain does not require a single “out of place” segment.
Clinical loading problems are usually better understood through:
- sensitivity / irritability
- movement tolerance
- exposure history
- deconditioning or capacity mismatch
- psychosocial and behavioural context

### 3. Discs and nerve roots are not the whole story
Lumbar symptoms may arise from:
- non-specific mechanical low back pain
- radicular pain / nerve root irritation
- spinal stenosis / neurogenic claudication
- referred hip or SIJ symptoms
- non-MSK pathology

### 4. Imaging findings are common and often poorly correlated with symptoms
Degenerative findings, disc bulges, and other structural changes are common with ageing and do not automatically define the pain source.

## Common symptom patterns
### Predominantly axial lumbar pain
Often:
- local lumbar or lumbosacral pain
- variable stiffness / guarding
- aggravated by bending, lifting, prolonged sitting or standing, transitions
- no clear dermatomal pain pattern

### Lumbar-related leg pain / sciatica
Often:
- buttock and leg pain greater than or as important as back pain
- possible below-knee distribution
- neural symptoms: tingling, numbness, shooting / electric pain
- provocation with neural loading, cough / sneeze, sitting, flexion, or a specific movement pattern

### Lumbar spinal stenosis / neurogenic claudication pattern
Often:
- older adult presentation
- standing / walking intolerance
- leg symptoms with extension-biased loading
- relief with sitting or flexion-based positions

## Clinical implications
- Start with triage: rule out serious pathology and urgent neurological compromise.
- Then classify the dominant clinical problem:
  - non-specific low back pain
  - radicular pain / sciatica
  - stenosis pattern
  - adjacent-joint contributor
  - non-MSK source
- Use symptom behaviour and function, not imaging alone, to guide rehab.

## High-value examination domains
- symptom distribution and irritability
- repeated / sustained loading tolerance
- neurological screen when indicated
- hip contribution screen
- walking, transfers, sitting, lifting, sleep, work tolerance
- psychosocial obstacles to recovery
