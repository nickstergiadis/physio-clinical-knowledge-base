# Lumbar Clinical Reasoning

## Purpose
This page is the bridge between lumbar anatomy and lumbar condition pages.
Use it to decide what bucket the patient fits into first, then refine management.

## Step 1: triage first
Immediately think about:
- cauda equina / severe progressive neurological deficit
- fracture risk / trauma
- infection
- malignancy
- inflammatory spinal disease
- abdominal / pelvic / renal / vascular referral

## Step 2: identify the dominant presentation
### A. Non-specific low back pain is most likely when:
- pain is mainly axial lumbar / lumbosacral
- symptoms are mechanically variable
- no convincing progressive neurological deficit
- no clear alternative diagnosis explains the whole picture

### B. Lumbar radicular pain / sciatica is more likely when:
- leg pain is dominant or at least highly clinically relevant
- symptoms travel in a nerve-root pattern or behave like neural pain
- there may be numbness / tingling / weakness
- cough, sneeze, sitting, neural loading, or repeated movements may provoke symptoms

### C. Spinal stenosis pattern is more likely when:
- standing / walking tolerance is limited
- symptoms ease with sitting / flexion
- age and presentation fit a degenerative narrowing pattern

### D. Adjacent-region driver is more likely when:
- lumbar findings are underwhelming
- hip, SIJ, or non-MSK signs better explain the complaint

## Step 3: assess irritability
Ask:
- how easily are symptoms triggered?
- how severe do they get?
- how long do they take to settle?
- what does a flare look like over 24 hours?

High irritability changes dosing more than diagnosis.

## Step 4: identify modifiable contributors
Common contributors:
- acute load spike
- low recent activity / deconditioning
- sleep disruption
- fear / avoidance
- high distress / low confidence
- work exposure mismatch
- prolonged static postures
- reduced hip capacity / movement options

## Step 5: decide what treatment needs to accomplish first
Common initial priorities:
- reduce threat / uncertainty
- keep the patient moving
- find tolerable movement windows
- regain basic functional tolerance
- restore strength / endurance / walking capacity
- reduce overreliance on passive care and medication

## Useful framing statements
- “The goal is not perfect posture; it is better tolerance and confidence.”
- “Imaging findings often do not map neatly to pain.”
- “A flare is not automatically harm.”
- “We will dose movement to your irritability, not force through it.”

## Escalation triggers
Escalate or refer when:
- serious pathology is suspected
- neurological deficit is worsening
- diagnosis remains unclear
- disability remains high after a well-structured rehab trial
- severe sciatica persists with concordant imaging / specialist indications
