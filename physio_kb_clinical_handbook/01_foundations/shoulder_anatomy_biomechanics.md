# Shoulder Anatomy + Biomechanics

## Clinical summary
The shoulder complex trades bony constraint for mobility. Clinical reasoning therefore has to account for both pain-generating tissues and the movement / load-sharing system around the glenohumeral joint, scapulothoracic articulation, acromioclavicular joint, sternoclavicular joint, thoracic cage, and cervicothoracic region.

## Core anatomical components
- glenohumeral joint
- scapulothoracic articulation
- acromioclavicular (AC) joint
- sternoclavicular (SC) joint
- thoracic spine / rib cage contribution
- rotator cuff tendons and capsule
- long head of biceps tendon
- labrum and capsuloligamentous restraints

## Key stabilizers
### Static stabilizers
- glenoid version / depth
- labrum
- capsule
- glenohumeral ligaments
- negative intra-articular pressure
- bony shape

### Dynamic stabilizers
- rotator cuff
- scapular muscles
- deltoid
- long head of biceps
- trunk and lower-limb contribution during higher demand tasks

## High-yield biomechanics
- shoulder elevation depends on glenohumeral motion plus scapular upward rotation, posterior tilt, and external rotation
- rotator cuff and deltoid work together to center the humeral head while producing elevation torque
- load transfer from trunk to hand depends on clavicle + scapula motion as well as thoracic extension / rotation capacity
- loss of passive range in multiple planes, especially external rotation, raises suspicion for adhesive capsulitis rather than routine rotator cuff-related pain
- instability presentations require thinking about both static restraint failure and insufficient dynamic control

## Region links that matter clinically
- cervical referral and neural contribution can mimic shoulder pain
- thoracic stiffness may influence overhead tolerance
- AC joint and SC joint may be pain sources even when the patient reports “shoulder pain”
- posterior shoulder symptoms can reflect cuff / labrum / instability / referred pain rather than one single structure

## Tissue-specific pattern reminders
### Rotator cuff-related shoulder pain
Commonly presents with:
- painful arc or painful elevation
- resisted cuff loading sensitivity
- load-related irritability
- night pain / lying-on-shoulder aggravation in some patients

### Adhesive capsulitis
Commonly presents with:
- marked passive loss of external rotation and often abduction / flexion
- painful stiffness
- dressing / hand-behind-back limits
- slow onset and prolonged course

### Traumatic anterior instability
Commonly presents with:
- trauma history / dislocation or subluxation
- apprehension in abduction-external rotation ranges
- younger / contact / collision athlete pattern more common
- recurrence concern and activity-specific fear

## Implications for rehab planning
- do not treat all shoulder pain as “cuff weakness”
- use symptom behavior plus passive / active patterning to separate pain-dominant from stiffness-dominant and instability-dominant presentations
- overhead rehab often needs scapular, cuff, thoracic, and kinetic-chain work, not isolated shoulder exercises alone
- return-to-sport planning needs force absorption, rate-of-force development, and psychological readiness in addition to pain reduction
