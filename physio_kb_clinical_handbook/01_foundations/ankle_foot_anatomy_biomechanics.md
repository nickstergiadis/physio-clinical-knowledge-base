# Ankle + Foot Anatomy / Biomechanics

## Clinical purpose
This page is a quick-reference foundation for common outpatient ankle and posterior heel presentations in this KB, especially:
- lateral ankle sprain (LAS)
- chronic ankle instability (CAI)
- midportion Achilles tendinopathy

## Core regions to keep separate clinically
### Talocrural joint
Primary motions:
- dorsiflexion
- plantarflexion

Clinical notes:
- often the main source of painful post-sprain stiffness
- dorsiflexion loss commonly persists after LAS
- anterior-to-posterior talar glide limits may affect squat, stair descent, and running mechanics

### Subtalar joint
Primary motions:
- inversion / eversion contribution
- frontal-plane adaptation to uneven ground

Clinical notes:
- often relevant in recurrent sprain and balance deficits
- contributes to foot loading strategy and dynamic control

### Distal tibiofibular syndesmosis
Clinical notes:
- consider when mechanism, pain distribution, and weight-bearing difficulty are disproportionate to a typical lateral sprain
- pain often higher than the lateral ligaments and may increase with external rotation / dorsiflexion stress

### Lateral ligament complex
Main structures:
- anterior talofibular ligament (ATFL)
- calcaneofibular ligament (CFL)
- posterior talofibular ligament (PTFL)

Clinical notes:
- ATFL is most commonly involved in inversion / plantarflexion sprain mechanisms
- CFL involvement becomes more likely with greater inversion load / severity
- PTFL injury is less common and usually associated with major trauma or dislocation patterns

### Achilles tendon
Relevant distinctions:
- midportion tendinopathy: usually 2–6 cm proximal to insertion
- insertional Achilles pain: clinically different and should not be managed exactly the same as midportion presentations

Clinical notes:
- tendon loading tolerance, calf capacity, and symptom irritability are more useful than imaging alone for management planning
- tendon thickening may be present but does not define severity by itself

## Functional anatomy that matters in rehab
### Calf complex
- gastrocnemius contributes strongly to propulsion and higher-load plantarflexion work
- soleus is critical for endurance, stance control, and running economy
- weakness or deconditioning often contributes to persistent Achilles symptoms and reduced ankle function after sprain

### Peroneal muscles
- dynamic lateral ankle support
- rapid response to inversion moments
- often deconditioned or poorly recruited after recurrent sprain

### Tibialis posterior + foot intrinsics
- support medial arch / foot control
- influence overall foot and ankle load distribution
- can matter when distal control is poor or pronation strategy is excessive / poorly controlled

## Biomechanical themes by condition
### Lateral ankle sprain
Common mechanism:
- inversion, often with plantarflexion

Typical consequences:
- pain / swelling
- dorsiflexion loss
- altered gait / reduced stance time
- reduced confidence with single-leg loading
- sensorimotor deficits that may persist beyond tissue healing

### Chronic ankle instability
Usually characterized by:
- recurrent sprains and/or repeated “giving way”
- persistent perceived instability
- balance / proprioceptive deficits
- reduced dynamic control under cutting, landing, uneven-ground, or sport demand

Biomechanical emphasis:
- not just laxity
- often includes sensorimotor, neuromuscular, strength, and movement-coordination deficits

### Midportion Achilles tendinopathy
Usual loading context:
- running, jumping, hill work, or other repeated tendon-loading exposure
- may also occur in less active individuals with abrupt load spikes or deconditioning

Biomechanical emphasis:
- tendon pain is generally load related
- calf endurance / plantarflexor capacity matter
- abrupt volume spikes, low recovery capacity, and underprepared tissue load are common contributors

## Region-specific reasoning reminders
- do not over-interpret MRI or ultrasound changes without matching symptoms and exam findings
- a “simple sprain” can still leave meaningful deficits if dorsiflexion, balance, and confidence are not restored
- not all posterior heel pain is midportion Achilles tendinopathy; insertional pain, bursitis, partial tear, and referred pain need separation
- the lumbar spine, proximal calf, and neurovascular structures occasionally mimic distal ankle/foot symptoms and should be considered when symptoms are atypical

## High-yield objective domains
- dorsiflexion mobility (including weight-bearing lunge-type testing)
- swelling / irritability
- single-leg balance / dynamic balance
- calf raise capacity
- hopping / deceleration tolerance when appropriate
- gait and stair tolerance
- confidence / fear under load
