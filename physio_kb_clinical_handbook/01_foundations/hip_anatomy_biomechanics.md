# Hip Anatomy + Biomechanics

## Core anatomy
- ball-and-socket joint formed by the femoral head and acetabulum
- acetabular labrum contributes to depth, seal, and load distribution
- capsule and key passive restraints include iliofemoral, pubofemoral, and ischiofemoral ligaments
- major force contributors: gluteals, deep rotators, iliopsoas, adductors, hamstrings, quadriceps

## Practical biomechanics
- the hip transfers load between trunk and lower limb
- flexion, adduction, and internal rotation often become symptom-sensitive positions in FAIS-type presentations
- frontal-plane control and hip abductor function matter for gait, stairs, single-leg tasks, and lateral hip loading
- hip extension capacity influences stride length, running mechanics, and lumbopelvic compensation
- loss of rotation, especially internal rotation, is clinically relevant in hip OA and FAIS patterns

## Common clinical patterns
- groin-dominant pain: think intra-articular drivers, hip flexor region, adductor contribution, or referral
- lateral hip pain: think GTPS, gluteal tendon compression/load intolerance, lumbar referral, or less commonly OA referral
- buttock pain: consider deep gluteal structures, lumbar referral, SIJ-adjacent pain, hamstring origin, or hip capsule pattern

## High-value movement tasks
- gait
- sit-to-stand
- squat / hinge
- step-up / step-down
- single-leg stance
- hip flexion-adduction-IR provocation
- rotation and extension tolerance

## Clinical implications
- do not over-interpret static posture alone
- combine symptom location, ROM pattern, loading response, and function
- always screen lumbar spine and, when relevant, adductor / abdominal / pelvic contributors
