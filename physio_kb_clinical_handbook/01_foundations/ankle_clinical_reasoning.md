# Ankle Clinical Reasoning

## Why this page exists
Most outpatient ankle presentations are not hard because of anatomy alone. They are hard because the clinician has to separate:
- acute tissue irritation vs persistent load intolerance
- instability perception vs true recurrent sprain risk
- tendon pain vs insertional / bursitic / neural / bony causes
- low-risk sprain vs fracture / syndesmosis / osteochondral / tendon rupture patterns

## First split: what broad bucket is this?
### 1. Acute lateral ankle sprain pattern
Think of this when there is:
- clear inversion or inversion/plantarflexion mechanism
- lateral swelling / bruising / tenderness
- painful weight-bearing, walking, stairs, change of direction
- reduced dorsiflexion and reduced confidence early

### 2. Chronic ankle instability pattern
Think of this when there is:
- history of previous ankle sprain(s)
- repeated “giving way” or recurrent sprains
- uneven-ground or sport apprehension
- persistent balance / coordination deficits
- activity restriction despite “healed” ligaments

### 3. Midportion Achilles tendinopathy pattern
Think of this when there is:
- localized pain 2–6 cm proximal to insertion
- gradual onset or load-spike onset
- pain with running, hopping, hills, skipping, calf raises, or push-off
- focal tenderness / possible thickening
- relatively low value of passive stretching tests compared with loaded tendon provocation

## Second split: what makes the usual diagnosis less likely?
### For a “simple lateral ankle sprain,” reconsider when there is:
- inability to bear weight with bony tenderness suggesting fracture
- pain disproportionate to a mild mechanism
- marked proximal/distal pain away from the usual ligament zone
- syndesmosis mechanism or high ankle pain
- locking / catching / persistent deep joint pain suggesting osteochondral injury
- Achilles rupture signs
- neurovascular symptoms

### For CAI, reconsider when there is:
- pain dominance without instability history
- rigid mechanical block
- inflammatory pattern
- gross osteochondral / degenerative change dominating the picture
- proximal driver (lumbar / hip / central balance issue) that better explains the symptoms

### For Achilles tendinopathy, reconsider when there is:
- insertional rather than midportion pain
- sudden “pop” / acute weakness / rupture concern
- diffuse swelling, redness, or systemic concern
- pain that is not clearly load related
- neural symptoms or marked morning inflammatory behavior out of proportion to tendon loading

## Triage / red-flag style questions
- Can they weight-bear four steps?
- Is there bony tenderness at the malleoli / base of fifth metatarsal / navicular?
- Was there a sudden snap or pop in the Achilles with major push-off weakness?
- Is swelling hot, red, or associated with systemic illness?
- Is calf pain/swelling raising DVT concern?
- Are symptoms severely disproportionate or worsening unexpectedly?

## Examination logic
### In acute LAS
Prioritize:
- fracture screening / Ottawa-style reasoning
- swelling / irritability
- weight-bearing tolerance
- ROM, especially dorsiflexion
- progressive reassessment rather than overly aggressive special testing early

### In CAI
Prioritize:
- history of previous sprain and giving-way episodes
- dynamic balance
- dorsiflexion mobility
- peroneal / calf / whole-limb capacity
- sport or activity-specific movement coordination
- confidence, fear, and return-to-sport readiness

### In Achilles tendinopathy
Prioritize:
- symptom location
- tendon loading response
- calf raise capacity / endurance
- hop tolerance if appropriate
- recent training / loading errors
- separation from insertional pathology or rupture

## Common management errors to avoid
- immobilizing too long after a routine lateral sprain
- focusing only on swelling/pain and never rebuilding balance + strength
- calling all persistent post-sprain symptoms “ligament laxity”
- over-resting Achilles tendinopathy instead of restoring pain-tolerable loading
- progressing Achilles loading too fast after symptoms settle
- using imaging findings as the main diagnosis instead of the clinical picture

## Quick documentation language
### Acute LAS
“Presentation most consistent with lateral ankle sprain with [low/moderate/high] irritability, currently limited by pain, swelling, dorsiflexion restriction, and reduced weight-bearing confidence; no current strong indicators of fracture / syndesmosis / Achilles rupture after screening.”

### CAI
“Presentation most consistent with chronic ankle instability, with recurrent giving way / recurrent sprain history plus persistent balance, load tolerance, and movement-coordination deficits; plan is sensorimotor and strength-focused rehab with graded return to higher-demand tasks.”

### Midportion Achilles tendinopathy
“Presentation most consistent with midportion Achilles tendinopathy, with localized load-related tendon pain and reduced calf capacity; management will prioritize progressive tendon loading, activity modification, and capacity rebuilding rather than rest alone.”
