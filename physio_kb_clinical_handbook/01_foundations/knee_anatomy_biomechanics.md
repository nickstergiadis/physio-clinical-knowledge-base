# Knee Anatomy + Biomechanics

## Why this page exists
Use this page to anchor clinical reasoning before jumping to diagnosis labels. Knee pain is often driven by the interaction of:
- tibiofemoral load tolerance
- patellofemoral joint stress
- meniscal and capsuloligamentous contribution
- hip / trunk / ankle mechanics
- training, occupational, and bodyweight-related load exposure

## Structural overview
### Tibiofemoral joint
- primary hinge joint with coupled roll / glide and axial rotation
- medial and lateral compartments differ in shape and load behavior
- extension is often the most sensitive “must-have” range for gait and function

### Patellofemoral joint
- patella increases quadriceps moment arm and modifies extensor mechanics
- patellofemoral compressive load increases with deeper flexion and with high-demand knee extensor tasks
- symptom response depends on both tissue irritability and current load tolerance

### Menisci
- improve load distribution, shock absorption, joint stability, and proprioceptive function
- degenerative meniscal findings on imaging become more common with age and do not automatically explain symptoms
- clinical relevance depends on history, exam behavior, irritability, and competing diagnoses

### Passive stabilizers
- ACL / PCL
- MCL / LCL / posterolateral structures
- capsule and retinacular structures

### Active stabilizers
- quadriceps
- hamstrings
- gastrocnemius
- gluteals / trunk as proximal contributors to lower-limb control

## Biomechanical themes that matter clinically
### Load tolerance
Symptoms often reflect a mismatch between joint/tissue capacity and recent load:
- walking volume
- stairs / inclines
- squatting / kneeling
- running and jumping
- occupational kneeling, lifting, carrying, and repeated sit-to-stand

### Patellofemoral loading
Patellofemoral symptoms often respond to:
- step-down / squat mechanics
- training spikes
- changes in running, hills, speed, or footwear
- deconditioning of knee and hip musculature

### Tibiofemoral OA loading
Knee OA commonly presents with:
- activity-related pain
- stiffness after rest
- reduced confidence / reduced activity
- tolerance limits with stairs, longer walking, sit-to-stand, and kneeling
The goal is usually not to “offload forever,” but to rebuild tolerable loading capacity.

### Meniscal symptom behavior
Meniscal presentations are more convincing when there is:
- a compatible trauma or loaded twist history
- localized joint-line pain
- effusion or delayed swelling
- pain with loaded flexion / rotation
- intermittent mechanical block / locking that is clinically meaningful
Degenerative meniscal change often coexists with OA and may not require a surgery-first mindset.

## Linked-region contributors
Always consider:
- hip strength / control
- trunk control and single-leg mechanics
- ankle dorsiflexion and foot loading pattern
- gait strategy and walking tolerance
- bodyweight / cardiometabolic context when relevant

## Practical clinical implications
- Do not over-interpret imaging findings in isolation.
- Prioritize symptom behavior, function, irritability, and load response.
- Build management around the dominant clinical syndrome:
  - knee OA
  - patellofemoral pain
  - degenerative meniscal presentation
  - acute traumatic instability / large effusion / true locking / fracture concern
- Use active rehab as the main pathway in most non-surgical knee presentations.
