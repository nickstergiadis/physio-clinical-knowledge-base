# Knee Clinical Reasoning

## Core question
Which knee presentation best explains the person in front of you, and what actually changes management?

## Common buckets
### 1. Knee osteoarthritis
Think higher probability when:
- age usually 45+
- activity-related joint pain
- little or no morning stiffness, or stiffness <30 minutes
- reduced walking / stairs / sit-to-stand tolerance
- crepitus may be present but is not essential
- imaging is often unnecessary for straightforward diagnosis

### 2. Patellofemoral pain
Think higher probability when:
- anterior or retropatellar pain
- younger or physically active presentation is common
- pain with squat, stairs, running, hills, prolonged sitting, or repeated knee flexion tasks
- no major effusion or true mechanical block
- symptoms often relate to recent load changes and movement tolerance deficits

### 3. Degenerative meniscal presentation
Think higher probability when:
- middle-aged or older adult
- joint-line dominant pain or loaded twist/flexion aggravation
- intermittent catching sensations may occur
- often overlaps with OA
- mechanical symptoms alone do not automatically justify surgery

### 4. Acute traumatic knee injury
Escalate suspicion when there is:
- large rapid effusion
- significant trauma
- inability to fully extend due to true block
- instability / giving way after trauma
- fracture concern or inability to weight-bear appropriately
This page is not the main pathway for acute major trauma / acute ligament rupture.

## What actually matters in the subjective
- exact pain location
- load triggers: stairs, squat, sit-to-stand, kneeling, running, twisting, hills
- irritability and 24-hour response
- swelling timing: immediate, delayed, absent
- locking: true block to motion versus painful catching
- instability: confidence issue versus mechanical giving way
- work / sport exposure
- prior injections / imaging / surgery
- expectations and fear around degeneration, cartilage, or “bone on bone”

## What matters in the objective exam
- gait
- swelling / effusion
- active and passive extension / flexion
- squat and sit-to-stand tolerance
- step-down / step-up
- single-leg loading
- hip and trunk control
- knee extensor strength and pain response
- targeted meniscal / ligament / patellofemoral provocation only when it changes reasoning

## Imaging logic
### Usually not routine first-line
- straightforward knee OA presentation
- classic patellofemoral pain without red flags
- degenerative meniscal presentations where management will still begin conservatively

### More useful when
- fracture or major trauma is suspected
- true locked knee is present
- major ligament injury is suspected
- persistent diagnostic uncertainty remains after exam
- surgical planning is being considered

## Treatment hierarchy
### Most knee presentations in this cluster
Start with:
- education
- load modification, not load avoidance
- progressive exercise
- weight management support when relevant
- adjuncts only if they help participation

### Adjuncts
May be useful selectively:
- patellar taping
- foot orthoses in selected PFP presentations
- walking aids / bracing in selected OA presentations
- short-term medication support coordinated with medical guidance

### Escalation
Consider referral when:
- there is suspected major structural injury
- severe OA remains highly limiting despite adequate conservative care
- true locking or major extension block persists
- symptoms remain disabling and diagnosis or pathway is unclear
